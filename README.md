My research question for this project reads as follows: How do the regional distribution and motivations of extralegal collective violence from 1783–1865 relate to later patterns of violent crime and potential racialized violence in the United States? To answer this question, I combined quantitative and qualitative methods across three datasets. 

First, I analyzed a dataset of extralegal violence incidents from 1783–1865, ranking regions by frequency and using text analysis to identify dominant motivations, with particular attention to racial language. After calculating the rate of extralegal violence in the year 1850, I then compared these historical patterns to regional violent crime rates in 1977. Finally, I incorporated a modern dataset on police shootings, filtering for cases involving unarmed Black individuals, as a proxy for examining the potential persistence of racially motivated violence. 

The findings reveal both change and continuity. From 1783–1865, the West had the highest number of extralegal and racially motivated incidents, reflecting the instability of frontier expansion, weak formal institutions, and competition over land and resources. Surprisingly, the South did not have the highest overall rates of violence, but violence there was more consistently tied to race and slavery. This distinction between frequency and motivation is important to note. By 1977, the South had the highest violent crime rates, suggesting a geographic shift in the concentration of violence over time. However, when focusing specifically on racially motivated violence, a clearer pattern of continuity emerges. During the historical period, the South’s violence was deeply embedded in systems of racial hierarchy and control. Modern data showing a higher concentration of police shootings involving unarmed Black individuals in the South (rather than the West, as the historic dataset depicted) suggests that these patterns may continue, although in different forms. This does not imply a direct equivalence between historical extralegal violence and contemporary law enforcement, but it does point to the possibility that underlying racial dynamics have become institutionalized rather than disappeared. Overall, the project demonstrates that while the location and scale of violence can shift significantly over time, the motivations behind that violence can endure and reshape themselves within new social and institutional contexts.
